There are some critical points I'd like to clarify on this matter:

First: Let's Define Slip, Lapse, and Relapse

  • Slip: A momentary lapse, like looking at an image or a scene and quickly moving away.
  • Lapse: Returning to old habits, such as watching or engaging in inappropriate behaviors without abandoning the recovery path, typically only once, and then continuing the recovery journey.
  • Relapse: A full-blown return to old behaviors for an extended period, often several days.

The purpose of these definitions is to understand that your brain and recovery aren't as affected by a slip as they are by a relapse.

But what's the real issue?

The problem lies in how a recovering individual responds to these definitions.

A negative person might think, "Great, since watching or engaging once isn't considered a relapse but just a slip, I can do it and continue on."

Here's the Danger!

  • First: It's inherently sinful, and deliberately sinning is not permissible. Additionally, it trains your brain to give in, weakening your ability to resist temptations.
  • Second: Slips lead to depression, feelings of inferiority and shame, wasted time, and all the negative emotions you know follow them.
  • Third: Slips accumulate and can lead to a full relapse, which could last indefinitely.
  • Fourth: Slips delay recovery. If you're not progressing, you're undoubtedly regressing, even if it's not by much. You are still falling short of your goal to quit pornography entirely.
  • Fifth: Even a brief glance at an inappropriate image can revive the addiction within you, feeding it the "juice" it thrives on. So imagine the impact of repeated slips!

We must distinguish between what could happen and what will happen.

Could a slip happen? Yes, it could.

Could a lapse happen? Yes, it could.

Could a relapse happen? Yes, it could.

But if you internally believe that you will inevitably relapse, that's where the danger lies! A person who fights their enemy with the certainty that they will lose is defeated before even engaging in the battle.

Therefore, we must not use the possibility of a relapse as an excuse to be weak and give in.
